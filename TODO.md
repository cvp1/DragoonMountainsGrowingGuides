# DMR Growing Guides — Library Backlog

A running list of topics, refinements, and content gaps to fold into the
library. Captured as they surface in conversation or in use.

**Reference:** [PRINCIPLES.md](PRINCIPLES.md) — every item in this
backlog exists to serve the north-star objective: a well-rounded series
of guides that are easy to adopt and learn from, instructional enough
for a beginner to follow, and deep enough to answer expert-level
questions.

**Format:** each entry has a short heading, a capture date, brief notes,
a *Why it matters* line tying it to the principles, and a suggested home
in the library. Sections are grouped by type, not priority — pick what
speaks to you.

### A1 · Cucumbers [INTEGRATED in Guide 7 · §5 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-15*

Currently get only a single-line mention in **Guide 1 §10** ("Crop
selection beyond the named guides"). The conversation surfaced enough
material for at least a 2-page section, possibly a chapter of a new
guide.

Notes worth folding in:

- **Soil minimum 65°F** at planting time (vs 60°F for zucchini); soil
  typically clears that threshold in the Dragoons around mid-May.
- **Direct-sow window:** May 15 spring crop; second sowing early July
  for fall crop running Sep–Oct once monsoon humidity drops.
- **Trellis on cattle panel** — straighter fruit, better airflow against
  monsoon-driven powdery mildew.
- **Drip irrigation only** — never overhead.
- **Cucumber beetle management is the real challenge** in Cochise County
  (not heat). Striped and spotted beetles vector bacterial wilt, which
  kills the plant. Floating row cover until flowering, then hand-pick or
  pyrethrin at dusk.
- **Variety recommendations:**
  - Asian / Burpless types (*Suyo Long*, *Tasty Jade*) — handle Cochise
    heat best; never get bitter
  - *Marketmore 76* — reliable American slicer
  - *Boston Pickling* and *Calypso F1* — pickling, heavy yields
- **Heat threshold:** 95°F shuts down fruit set.

*Why it matters:* a popular beginner crop the library currently doesn't
serve. Beginners trying to grow cukes get nothing from us.

*Suggested home:* expand **Guide 1 §10** with a half-page profile, OR
spin out a **"Warm-season companions to peppers"** guide covering
cucumbers + squash + melons + eggplant (see B1).

### A2 · Zucchini & summer squash [INTEGRATED in Guide 7 · §6 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-15*

Same pattern as cucumbers — no dedicated section, only a brief mention.

Notes worth folding in:

- **Plant May 15** in the Dragoons (soil 60°F+).
- **Harvest timeline:** first fruit ~July 1; heavy through July; tapers
  in August (90°F+ slows fruit set).
- **Second sowing July 25 – August 5** for fall crop (Sep–Oct).
- **Squash vine borer** — wrap lower 6 inches of stem in aluminum foil
  at transplant.
- **Powdery mildew** with monsoon humidity (same management as cukes).
- **Variety recommendations:**
  - *Costata Romanesco* — Italian ribbed type, holds quality even
    oversized
  - *Black Beauty* — reliable open-pollinated standard
- **Cochise County easily supports two successions a year.**

*Why it matters:* same as cucumbers — common beginner crop, currently
unaddressed.

*Suggested home:* same as A1.

### A3 · Tomatoes [INTEGRATED in Guide 7 · §3 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Currently mentioned in **Guide 4 Part 1** (Masterblend feeding chart),
**Guide 1 §10** (one-line profile), and obliquely in **Guide 3 §7**
(adapting bato bucket for tomatoes). No coherent grow guide.

Tomatoes are probably the *single most-grown crop* by Cochise County
gardeners and yet the library treats them as an afterthought.

Material to gather:

- **Variety reference** — determinate vs indeterminate; cherry vs
  saladette vs slicer vs paste; heat-tolerant varieties (*Phoenix*,
  *Heatmaster*, *Sun Gold*, *Juliet*); heirlooms that work at elevation
  (*Black Krim*, *Cherokee Purple*).
- **Spring vs fall window** — Cochise lets you grow tomatoes twice
  (spring crop set before May heat, fall crop set after September
  cool-down). This is unusual and deserves its own treatment.
- **Heat protection** — 30% shade cloth above 95°F prevents blossom
  drop; mulch heavily.
- **Pruning** — single-leader vs multi-leader for indeterminates;
  Florida-weave training in bato buckets.
- **Common pests** — hornworm (hand-pick), psyllid (tobacco-mosaic
  virus vector), splitting (water management).
- **Soil vs hydroponic** — bato bucket tomatoes are excellent in the
  Dragoons; reservoir tuning differs slightly from peppers (more K
  during fruiting; covered in Guide 4 already).

*Why it matters:* major gap. Library claims to cover warm-season but
omits the most popular warm-season crop. Violates "well-rounded."

*Suggested home:* a dedicated **Guide 7 — Tomatoes in the Dragoons**,
mirroring Guide 2's soil + hydroponic structure. OR fold into a
"Warm-season companions" guide (see B1) as one of multiple crops.

### A4 · Herbs [INTEGRATED as Guide 8 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Visible in **Pictures** (the Kitchen Herb Bench photo) but not
addressed in any guide. Herbs are forgiving, container-friendly,
hydroponic-friendly — exactly the kind of crop where the library could
serve beginners well.

Material to gather:

- **Annual vs perennial split** — basil/cilantro/dill/parsley are
  short-season annuals; rosemary/thyme/sage/oregano/mint are perennials
  in zone 8a if sited correctly.
- **The bolt problem** — cilantro bolts in 6 weeks of any warmth; basil
  hates cold. Window management matters.
- **Hydroponic suitability** — basil and mint are NFT champions; cilantro
  works in DWC; parsley moderate. Woody perennials (rosemary, sage) are
  soil-only.
- **Mediterranean herbs are the best perennial fit for the Dragoons** —
  thyme, oregano, sage, rosemary all thrive on neglect with caliche
  drainage.
- **Drying & storage** — most herbs dry beautifully in the Dragoons'
  low humidity.

*Why it matters:* high-value crop for beginners (low investment, fast
reward); also the obvious companion to peppers in any kitchen garden.

*Suggested home:* could be a **Guide 8 — Herbs in the Dragoons**, or
folded into the warm-season companions guide (B1). Mediterranean
perennials could alternatively join the cool-season guide given they
grow year-round.

### A5 · Melons [INTEGRATED in Guide 7 · §7 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Mentioned in **Guide 1 §10** as a "future consideration"; no detail
anywhere.

Material to gather:

- **Variety:** *Hale's Best Jumbo* (cantaloupe); *Crimson Sweet*
  (watermelon); *Charentais* (French heirloom, exceptional in dry heat).
- **Timing:** May 15 direct-sow same window as cukes/zukes; melons need
  the full season and don't second-succession well.
- **Trellising small melons** (Charentais, Sugar Baby) saves space and
  improves quality.
- **Heat-loving** — Cochise heat is an asset for melons; sweetness
  develops with diurnal temperature swing.

*Why it matters:* a warm-season crop that genuinely thrives here;
underserved.

*Suggested home:* warm-season companions guide (B1).

### A6 · Sweet corn [INTEGRATED in Guide 7 · §9 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Not addressed anywhere. Cochise County has heritage with corn (Hopi,
Tohono O'odham). Worth a section.

Material to gather:

- **Variety types** — sugary (su), sugary enhanced (se), supersweet
  (sh2), synergistic (sy) — and the isolation requirements.
- **Heritage varieties:** Hopi blue corn, Painted Mountain, O'odham
  60-day, Bloody Butcher.
- **Spring vs late-summer succession** — corn-earworm pressure peaks
  August so an early planting (April 15) avoids the worst.
- **Spacing requirements** for proper pollination (4×4 minimum block,
  not single rows).
- **Water demands** — high; corn is a poor fit for water-limited
  systems but fine in monsoon season.

*Why it matters:* a major regional crop with cultural depth; expert
readers would expect coverage.

*Suggested home:* warm-season companions guide (B1), or dedicated grain
crops guide (B4).

### A7 · Beans [INTEGRATED in Guide 7 · §8 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Not addressed. Beans are a Sonoran Desert staple — teparies are
indigenous to this exact region — and the library's lack of coverage is
a gap.

Material to gather:

- **Pole vs bush** — pole types (Kentucky Wonder, Rattlesnake) outyield
  bush types but need trellising.
- **Teparies** — *Phaseolus acutifolius*, native to Sonoran Desert;
  extremely drought-tolerant; brown and white forms.
- **Pinto, Anasazi, Hopi yellow** — heritage dry beans.
- **Snap beans for fresh eating** — Provider, Blue Lake, Romano types.
- **Beans fix nitrogen** — important crop rotation pivot point (great
  pre-brassica or pre-corn).

*Why it matters:* native crops + nitrogen fixation = obvious
inclusions for a place-anchored library. Mentioned in Guide 1 as a
heritage staple but undelivered.

*Suggested home:* warm-season companions guide (B1), or fold tepary +
heritage dry beans into a cool-season-rotation guide.

### A8 · Sweet potatoes [INTEGRATED in Guide 7 · §10 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Not addressed. Cochise County's long warm season actually makes sweet
potatoes work — they need 100+ days of heat — and the high diurnal
swing gives them exceptional flavor.

Material to gather:

- **Variety:** *Beauregard*, *Georgia Jet*, *Murasaki* (purple), *Hannah*
  (yellow). Slip propagation in spring.
- **Planting window:** late May after soil is warm.
- **Curing:** 80°F + 85% humidity for 10 days after harvest — critical
  for storage. Probably the trickiest part for Dragoons growers given
  our humidity.
- **Pests:** wireworms in newly broken ground; rotate.

*Why it matters:* high-flavor crop that exploits the Dragoons climate;
unusual enough that beginners need guidance to attempt it.

*Suggested home:* warm-season companions guide (B1).

### A9 · Eggplant [INTEGRATED in Guide 7 · §4 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Listed in **Guide 1 §10** as a 2-line profile; no detail. Eggplant is
a *Solanum* like peppers and tomatoes — likely most of the Guide 2
infrastructure transfers cleanly.

Material to gather:

- **Variety types** — Italian (Black Beauty, Rosa Bianca), Asian (Ping
  Tung, Orient Express), African (Striped Toga). Asian types do better
  in Cochise heat — less bitter, faster maturation.
- **Trellis or stake** — fruit weight tips plants over.
- **Flea beetle protection** — major pest at seedling stage; row cover
  early.
- **Same hydroponic setup as peppers** — fold into Guide 2 §6 or
  treat as Guide 2 sidebar.

*Why it matters:* third leg of the warm-season *Solanum* stool;
underserved.

*Suggested home:* warm-season companions guide (B1), or appendix to
Guide 2.

### A10 · Tree fruits & perennial fruit [INTEGRATED as Guide 9 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Currently nothing in the library beyond grapes (Guide 5). Cochise
County is at the sweet spot for several stone fruits and the
"high-desert classics" (fig, pomegranate, jujube). Pecans where water
allows.

Material to gather:

- **Site selection** — frost-pocket avoidance for spring-blooming
  stone fruits; cold-tolerant rootstocks for almonds and apricots.
- **Watering** — drip + deep watering routine; not the same as annuals.
- **Pruning** — open-vase vs central-leader by species.
- **Chill hours** — Dragoons get 700–900 hours; matters for variety
  selection.
- **Pomegranates and jujubes** — practically maintenance-free here;
  good first fruit tree.

*Why it matters:* a place-anchored library that omits the trees its
climate is famous for has a gap.

*Suggested home:* a dedicated **Guide 9 — Backyard Orchard in the
Dragoons** — bigger scope than a chapter; perennial topics deserve their
own treatment like grapes do.

### B2 · IPM master reference [INTEGRATED in Guide 10 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Pest and disease information is scattered across crop guides (peppers
in Guide 2, brassicas in Guide 6, grapes in Guide 5). Cross-cutting
pests (aphids, javelina, harlequin bug, powdery mildew, root-knot
nematodes) appear in 2–3 guides each with no shared baseline.

A standalone IPM reference would:

- Group pests by *what they attack* (sucking insects, chewing insects,
  fungal diseases, vertebrate pressure)
- Provide a single canonical treatment protocol per pest, referenced
  from each crop guide instead of duplicated
- Cover the Cochise County-specific players: harlequin bug, squash
  vine borer, packrat, javelina, gopher, cucumber beetle bacterial
  wilt, monsoon-season powdery mildew

*Why it matters:* serves the "depth for experts" goal — experts want
the full IPM reasoning chain. Also reduces duplication and drift
between crop guides.

*Suggested home:* **Guide 10 — IPM for the Dragoons**, or a long
appendix to Guide 1.

### B4 · Harvest & preservation reference [INTEGRATED as Guide 12 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Pepper drying lives in Guide 2 §9. Brassica per-crop harvest is in
Guide 6 §2.6. Grape Brix/TA targets are in Guide 5 §11. No shared
reference for preserving the harvest (canning, fermenting, freezing,
dehydration, lacto-fermentation).

A standalone preservation guide would close the loop from "I grew it"
to "we eat it through winter."

Topics:

- Canning safety (acidity, pressure vs water-bath, altitude
  adjustments — important at 4,700 ft)
- Lacto-fermentation (sauerkraut, kimchi, brined peppers, salsa)
- Drying (sun, oven, dehydrator, smoke) — Dragoons low humidity is
  ideal
- Freezing (which crops freeze well, blanching, vacuum-sealing)
- Root cellar / cool storage for fall roots
- Pepper-specific: ristra-making, smoking for chipotles, fermented
  hot sauces

*Why it matters:* the harvest is the point. A library that covers
growing but not preserving leaves the reader stranded in October.

*Suggested home:* **Guide 12 — Harvest & Preservation**.

### B5 · Wildlife pressure & exclusion [INTEGRATED in Guide 10 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Javelina, packrats, jackrabbits, deer, gopher, snake (yes — for vermin
control), and bird pressure are major Cochise County realities but get
only passing mentions in current guides. Worth a focused treatment.

Topics:

- **Bird netting** — when, how, and avoiding bird mortality
- **Snakes as garden allies** — gopher snake and king snake

- **Dogs as garden security** — practical notes

*Why it matters:* this is the kind of place-specific knowledge a
generic guide never has. High value for the "Dragoons voice" of the
library.

*Suggested home:* **Guide 13 — Wildlife & Exclusion**, or substantial
section in Guide 1.

### B6 · Composting & soil building [INTEGRATED in Guide 11 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Compost is referenced in Guide 2 §5.1 (pepper bed prep) and Guide 6
§2.1 (brassica bed prep) but never as its own topic. The Dragoons'
alkaline low-OM soil makes soil-building the single most impactful
long-term garden activity.

Topics:

- **Composting in dry climate** — water retention, browns vs greens
  with low natural humidity
- **Vermicomposting** — works year-round indoors
- **Bokashi** — fermentation-based, fast cycle
- **Sheet composting / lasagna beds** for new beds
- **Manure sourcing** — local horse / cattle / chicken; salt-loaded
  manures to avoid
- **Cover cropping for soil building** — winter rye, hairy vetch,
  buckwheat, crimson clover

*Why it matters:* serves "depth for experts" — soil-building is the
infinitely deep topic; also "easy to adopt" since a beginner with
better soil succeeds faster.

*Suggested home:* **Guide 14 — Composting & Soil Building** OR a long
chapter in Guide 1.

### B7 · Water sourcing & well primer [INTEGRATED as Guide 13 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Water source information lives scattered across Guide 3 §4
(bicarbonate protocol), Guide 4 Part 3 (pH chemistry rationale), Guide
4 §6 (water summary). No standalone treatment of *the water itself* —
where it comes from, how to test it, what the typical Cochise County
profile is, when an RO unit makes sense.

Topics:

- **Well testing** — what tests to run; where (UA Cooperative
  Extension); how to interpret results
- **Cochise water types** — Sulphur Springs Valley, San Pedro Valley,
  Dragoon Mountains — typical profiles
- **City water (Tombstone, Benson, Sierra Vista, Willcox)** —
  chlorine/chloramine treatment
- **Rainwater harvesting** — sizing, cisterns, first-flush diverters
- **RO units** — when justified (TDS > 500 ppm? hardness?), cost
  comparison, waste-water considerations

*Why it matters:* the foundation under everything else. Many readers
will get stuck on water before reaching nutrient or system choices.

*Suggested home:* **Guide 15 — Water in Cochise County** or a major
appendix to Guide 1.

### B8 · Greenhouse / hoop house construction [INTEGRATED as Guide 14 · 2026-05-16]
*Captured: 2026-05-16*

Mentioned obliquely in Guide 2 §10 (overwintering) and Guide 6 §3.7
(allium row cover) but no construction-side treatment. Season extension
in the Dragoons is hugely valuable — both for pepper overwintering and
for stretching brassica/lettuce window into May.

Topics:

- **Cold frames** — simplest; sized for one to four flats
- **Low tunnels** — hoops + row cover or 6-mil plastic
- **Hoop houses** — 12×24 standard; bow-pipe vs PVC vs steel
- **High tunnels** — NRCS cost-share programs in Arizona
- **Heating?** — usually not needed in zone 8a; passive solar gain
  + thermal mass is enough
- **Ventilation** — critical in spring/fall; automatic openers worth
  every dollar

*Why it matters:* an entire class of beginner-to-expert decisions
the library doesn't currently support. Construction guides reward
both first-timers (step-by-step) and experts (variations).

*Suggested home:* **Guide 16 — Season Extension Structures**.

## Tooling

### Picture sync workflow
*Captured: 2026-05-16 · INTEGRATED 2026-05-16*

The library now ships with `pictures-manifest.json` as the single
source of truth for gallery photo metadata, and `scripts/sync-pictures.py`
as the workflow tool.

**To add a new photo:**

1. Drop a `.jpeg` (or any size `.jpg`) into the `pictures/` folder.
2. Run `python3 scripts/sync-pictures.py` from the repository root.
   The script will resize the original to a web-optimized `.jpg`,
   stub an entry in `pictures-manifest.json`, and regenerate both
   the gallery section of `pictures.html` and the "From the Field"
   strip on `index.html`.
3. Edit the stub in `pictures-manifest.json` to fill in `category`,
   `title`, `short`, `full`, and set `featured: true` if the photo
   should appear on the homepage strip (only the first 3 featured
   photos in manifest order show up there).
4. Re-run `python3 scripts/sync-pictures.py` to regenerate HTML
   with your captions.
5. Commit and redeploy.

The script is idempotent: re-running it does nothing if there are no
new originals and no manifest changes.

Files involved:

- `pictures-manifest.json` — source of truth (excluded from deploy)
- `scripts/sync-pictures.py` — build-time tool (excluded from deploy)
- `pictures.html` — has `<!-- AUTOGEN-GALLERY:START/END -->` markers
- `index.html` — has `<!-- AUTOGEN-STRIP:START/END -->` markers
- `.dockerignore` and `.railwayignore` — exclude scripts/ and the
  manifest from the deployed image
